\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

Despite these results, several limitations merit discussion. First, the evaluation was conducted on a single curated dataset; the generalization of learned quality priors across diverse demographic distributions and sensor types remains to be validated. Second, the current framework fuses only two modalities (face and fingerprint); its extension to iris, finger-vein, or voice would require careful reconsideration of the tokenization strategy and cross-modal attention geometry. Third, the Monte Carlo Dropout mechanism incurs a multi-forward-pass overhead that increases inference latency by a factor equal to the number of stochastic samples, which may limit deployment on resource-constrained edge devices.

Future work will pursue four directions. \textit{(i)}\,Scale: curate and release larger multimodal benchmarks with demographic stratification and cross-sensor evaluation protocols. \textit{(ii)}\,Modality expansion: extend the token-based fusion architecture to iris and voice by designing domain-specific tokenizers and modality-aware quality embeddings. \textit{(iii)}\,Efficiency: apply knowledge distillation to compress the transformer backbone, investigate lightweight deterministic approximations of epistemic uncertainty (e.g., evidential learning) to eliminate the MC Dropout sampling cost, and explore federated deployment for privacy-preserving multimodal biometric matching across distributed clients. \textit{(iv)}\,Reliability: integrate conformal prediction techniques to provide distribution-free, sample-specific confidence intervals for verification decisions. UFM-Transformer offers a principled foundation for trustworthy multimodal biometrics---one where accuracy and calibrated confidence advance together rather than at each other's expense.
